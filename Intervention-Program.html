INTERVENTION-PROGRAM PLAN

1. Title of Project:

Assessing HIV status Among High school and college students in relation to HIV prevention

2. Author:

FNU Unseba

3. Problem / Need Statement

The young people in Cook County, Illinois (15-24 years old) have low HIV testing rates and stigma levels and minimal access to youth-friendly, confidential services. The adolescent population is the least tested, even though it constitutes 21% of new HIV diagnoses (CDC, 2023). Low perceived risk, misinformation, and fear of judgment are some of the factors that lead to delay in diagnosis and spread of the disease within the community. The youth in Cook County are disproportionately affected by African Americans and Latinos. A severe need is observed in a health communication intervention that raises awareness, decreases stigma, and facilitates the availability of testing resources (National Cancer Institute, 2002).

4. Goal(s)

Goal 1

To dramatically raise the number of adolescents and young adults (ages 15--24) who make HIV tests a regular practice in Cook County by actively increasing awareness, decreasing fear and misconceptions, and making information about testing more approachable, youth-sensitive, and culturally relevant (Glick et al., 2020).

Goal 2

To enhance the general health literacy on HIV among the youth in terms of misinformation through correcting myths, stigma reduction, and making testing an ordinary and routine part of healthy living as an adult.

5. Objectives

For Goal 1:

  • Turn around the proportions of youth, who are capable of naming at least two local locations that provide free so as well as confidential HIV testing, by 30% in half a year following program execution.
  • Increase the percentage of voluntary HIV testing participation to 20% among partner clinics, on-campus testing events, and CDPH mobile testing units within 12 months.

For Goal 2:

  • Increase the knowledge of HIV transmission, risk, and testing instructions among students by a quarter of what was observed at pre- and post-intervention surveys.
  • Lessen perceived HIV stigmatization, such as fear of being judged, embarrassment, or shame about sexual health by 15% following exposure to campaign messages and activities.

6. Sponsoring Agency / Contact Person

  • Chicago Department of Public Health (CDPH)
    HIV/STI Prevention Program Coordinator

7. Primary Target Audience(s)

High school and college students ages 15--24 in Cook County

Priority Characteristics:

  • Low HIV testing rates (9--15% have ever tested)
  • High reliance on social media (TikTok, Instagram, YouTube)
  • Diverse racial/ethnic backgrounds (African American, Latinx, Asian, immigrant youth)
  • Concerns about confidentiality
  • High mobile device usage; preference for short, visual content

8. Primary Audience Key Strategies

(Audience → Action/Message → Barriers → Benefits → Credentials → Channels)

Audience:

Youth in high school and college who are active users of digital media within Cook County and whose lifestyles rely on peer influence and mobile-based communications are aged 15 to 24.

Action/Message:

Promote youth volunteering by encouraging them to undergo testing as a means to get resulted by promoting HIV testing using the message:

"Know your status. Free, fast, confidential HIV testing is available near you. Taking control of your health is a smart, responsible, and empowering decision."

The messages emphasize the fact that testing is easy, confidential, noninvasive, and offered at places that appreciate the concerns of youth.

Barriers:

There are many challenges facing youth: fear of peer or adult judgment, uncertainty over whether the act is confidential, perceived low risk (it will not happen to me), ignorance of where to seek a test, and the cultural or family stigma of discussing sexual health. Others can also be concerned about the impact of a positive test or lack a complete understanding of HIV transmission.

Benefits:

Testing gives tranquility and empowerment, taking charge of one's health, early identification, and access to treatment where necessary. Messaging will also highlight that testing is a healthy and responsible health activity, just like physical check-ups; the privacy of people is safeguarded by confidentiality. It also shows how being aware of your health status helps to allow a healthy relationship and helps the community in its welfare.

Credentials:

Messages will be endorsed by the Chicago Department of Public Health, local health professionals, school-based health centers, youth peer ambassadors, and respected community youth organizations. Reliable and familiar sources are more credible, reassuring, and attractive to use.

Channels:

TikTok videos, Instagram Reels, Snapchat stories, YouTube shorts, digital posters, school-based announcements, and other communication platforms young people prefer. Peer ambassadors will communicate content using student groups, health clubs, and school events. Posters containing QR coded will be placed on campus, which will bring them to information about the testing sites.

9. Secondary Target Audience(s)

  1. Parents & caregivers: Influence attitudes and support for health decisions.
  2. School administrators & health educators: Key gatekeepers for school-based access.
  3. Community youth organizations: Provide safe spaces for outreach.

10. Secondary Audience Key Strategies

Audience: Parents & Caregivers

Action/Message:

Build their case about the need to facilitate their teen to receive accurate HIV information and regular testing as preventive health measures. Stress that HIV can potentially impact any youth with or without a background, and that an early diagnosis results in improved long-term health.

Barriers:

Cultural hesitation in discussing sexual health, the perception that their child is not sexually active, the stigma, and a lack of knowledge about HIV.

Benefits:

They are protecting the long-term health of their child, allowing early treatment in case of need, and encouraging supportive, open communication in families.

Credentials:

CDPH officers, pediatricians, and school nurses.

Channels:

Parent newsletters, PTA meetings, informational flyers, community workshops, Facebook groups.

Audience: School Administrators & Health Staff

Action/Message:

Influence alliances with CDPH to conduct on-campus testing events, include educating on HIV in health courses, and safe, confidential spaces where students can access information.

Barriers:

Parental resistance, school policy restrictions, time constraints, or unavailability of staff.

Benefits:

Good for student well-being, minimizes health disparities, improves school health achievement, and proactive school culture.

Channels:

Email outreach, online presentations, personnel meetings, and formal letters by CDPH.

11. Pretest Strategy

Test message and materials with a small sample group of 20-40 participating schools and colleges based on a sample of youth. Young people will go through sample TikToks, poster designs, and short messages. Clarity, relatability, cultural appropriateness, level of motivation, and a sense of a judgment-free tone will be assessed using focus groups and brief Internet surveys. Changes will be undertaken to make the communication sound natural and interesting to young people.

12. Theory Foundation - Health Belief Model (HBM)

The intervention applies the HBM to the beliefs and actions of youth:

  • Perceived Susceptibility: The messages clarify the actual statistics about the youth being vulnerable to HIV, outlining that most of the infected youth had thought they were not susceptible to it.
  • Perceived Severity: Short personal narratives show the long-term impacts of late diagnosis on health and life opportunities.
  • Perceived Benefits: Highlight the ease, secretiveness, and power derived through being aware of one's status.
  • Perceived Barriers: There is a clear explanation to minimize fear and stigma, particularly regarding privacy.
  • Action Cues: Reminders about social media, peer testimonials, and school-based reminders promote testing.
  • Self-Efficacy: Sometimes, tutorials and step-by-step instructions are used to allow the youth to feel confident in case they want to use testing services.

  • Required: Trained health educators, program coordinators, and peer ambassadors to implement workshops, coordinate testing events, manage communications, and monitor youth engagement.
  • Available: Chicago Department of Public Health (CDPH) staff will provide experienced HIV educators and program managers. Peer ambassadors will be recruited from participating high schools and colleges.
  • Sources: CDPH HIV/STI Prevention Program, school health clubs, student volunteer organizations.

2. Testing Equipment and Supplies

  • Required: HIV rapid test kits, gloves, sanitizer, sharps disposal containers, privacy screens, and mobile testing unit setup materials (tents, tables, chairs).
  • Available: CDPH provides rapid HIV test kits, clinical staff, and basic mobile unit supplies. Schools provide indoor/outdoor spaces for event setup.
  • Sources: CDPH mobile unit inventory, partner school facilities.

3. Educational and Promotional Materials

  • Required: Printed brochures, posters, banners, QR-coded cards linking to testing locations, and digital visuals for social media campaigns.
  • Available: CDPH provides standardized educational brochures; free online platforms (Canva, Adobe Express) will support design. Schools provide display areas and bulletin boards for printed materials.
  • Sources: CDPH educational resources, school facilities, and online content creation platforms.

4. Technology and Communication Tools

  • Required: Laptops, projectors, microphones, cameras for social media content creation, access to TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube accounts, and online survey platforms for evaluation.
  • Available: Program staff and CDPH offices supply laptops and projectors; schools provide access to classroom AV equipment; free or subscription-based online platforms will be used for social media campaigns.
  • Sources: CDPH IT resources, school technology departments, Canva/TikTok/Instagram platforms, SurveyMonkey/Qualtrics for surveys.

Personnel, Equipment, Supplies, Transportation, Subcontracts

1. Personnel

  • Program Staff & Educators: Includes CDPH health educators, program coordinators, and administrative staff responsible for planning, implementing, and evaluating workshops and outreach activities. They manage partnerships with schools, coordinate peer ambassadors, and oversee monthly testing events.
  • Peer Ambassadors: High school and college students recruited to serve as peer educators and social media advocates, promoting HIV testing among their peers. They assist with content dissemination, peer engagement, and event promotion.
  • Evaluation Team: Staff responsible for designing pre-/post-surveys, monitoring engagement, analyzing data, and preparing reports on program outcomes and impact.

2. Equipment

  • Digital Devices: Laptops, projectors, microphones, cameras, and smartphones are needed to produce, edit, and present educational content both in-person and online.
  • Mobile Testing Setup: Tents, tables, chairs, privacy screens, and signage for on-campus or community testing events.

3. Supplies

  • HIV Testing Supplies: Rapid HIV test kits, gloves, sanitizer, sharps disposal containers, and handouts.
  • Educational & Promotional Materials: Posters, brochures, flyers, QR-coded cards, banners, and other printed materials distributed across schools, youth centers, and public areas.

4. Transportation

  • CDPH Mobile Testing Unit: Fuel, maintenance, and vehicle use to transport staff, supplies, and testing equipment to schools and community events.
  • Staff Travel: Reimbursement for program staff or peer ambassadors traveling to schools, community sites, or meetings related to program implementation.

  • Media Content Creation: Contracts with designers or videographers to produce high-quality social media content (TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts) tailored to youth preferences.

15. Issues of Concern / Potential Problems

  1. Scheduling Conflicts
    Participation in workshops or testing events may be constrained by school events, exams, or holidays, or even sports schedules. Early coordination with administrators will be required, as well as flexibility.
  2. Policy Barriers
    HIV related programming might need parental approval in some schools, or sexual health issues may be limited in schools based on local policies or local administrator preference.
  3. Issues of Stigma and Cultural Sensitivity.
    In most societies, HIV-related stigma is still very pronounced, especially among youths of color and immigrant families. Stigma could decrease the readiness to participate in events or to use materials.
  4. Administrative Approval Delays.
    Schools have to accept the approval of the presence of CDPH teachers and mobile testing units. Delay due to bureaucracy may impact timelines, start dates, or program momentum.
  5. Privacy and Confidentiality Issues.
    Young people can fear their visibility when entering a testing field or addressing the health workers. It is essential to ensure confidential procedures and secret environments.
  6. Limited Staff Availability
    Staffing may be low and/or staff may be ill or have conflicting commitments, thus interfering with the effectiveness of regular testing events in CDPH.
  7. Technology Access Issues
    Students are not necessarily accessing the same social media platforms or have equivalent access to devices, which can limit the reach of digital campaigns.

16. Evaluation Strategies

A. Formative Evaluation

The following strategies take place during and before program development to make sure that materials are accommodative:

  • Pretesting Messages on the Youth
    Carry out focus groups and short surveys with a small sample of students to test wording, visuals, cultural sensitivity, and message credibility.
  • Pre-Launch Social Media Trials
    To gauge early engagement (views, likes, shares), a limited number of posts on TikTok and Instagram will be issued before the complete rollout.
  • CDPH and School Staff Feedback
    Materials will be reviewed by education experts and school nurses so that they can be health literate and appropriate in the school setting.
  • Pre-Launch Social Media Trials
    Observe one workshop in a chosen school to confirm the clarity, the involvement of students, and the logistical problems, and then proceed to all the sites.

B. Process Evaluation

The following strategies track program implementation:

  • Tracking Social Media Metrics
    Track views, shares, comments, likes, and hashtag activity to find out how the message reached and came across.
  • Monitoring Participation Numbers
    Document attendance recorded at each workshop, school presentation, and assessment of the success of outreach through testing.
  • Documenting Partnerships
    Monitor the involvement of schools, youth groups, and other community organisations.
  • Implementation Checklists
    Monthly checklists filled by the staff of the CDPH will contain the information about whether or not the tasks (content posting, testing events, workshops) were fulfilled on time.
  • Ongoing Youth Feedback
    Peer ambassadors will also report monthly about how students are responding to the campaign and give suggestions to improve it.

C. Summative Evaluation

Evaluates the effect and the results following the program.

Short-Term Effects (Outcome Evaluation)

  1. Compare before and after interventions surveys to evaluate changes in:
    • HIV knowledge
    • Knowledge of local testing places.
    • Perceptions of risk
    • Stigma levels
    • Belief in the availability of testing services.
  2. Determine whether youth express more intentions to get tested following program messages.

Impact Evaluation (Long-term Effects)

  1. Increases in real with actual HIV testing uptake with the CDPH clinic record, school testing event record, and the mobile unit statistics.
  2. Determine whether there was an increase in earlier HIV diagnosis as compared to the preceding year.
  3. Assessed the program decreased disparity in testing by racial and ethnic subgroups.
  4. Identify the existence of sustained relationships between schools and CDPH as a result of the program.
