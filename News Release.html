The seminar on Assessing HIV status Among High school and college students in relation to HIV prevention

Springfield, IL --- An educational seminar named Knowing Is Prevention: Assessing HIV Status Among High School and College Students will be conducted on December 6, 2025, between 10 a.m. and 2 p.m. at the Springfield Community Health Center. The incident is correlated with the Healthy People 2030 objective of helping to decrease new HIV cases as well as encouraging regular testing among adolescents and young adults.

The program will offer evidence-based information to the participants on HIV prevention and testing among the youth, which is clear. Recent statistics released by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC, 2024) indicate positive news on the national level: the estimated number of new HIV infections dropped by 12% during 2018-2022, and the number of new infections among people aged 13--24 years decreased by 30%. The enhancement is due to increased testing and preventive education in the country.

Even with this advancement, a large number of youths are oblivious of whether they are HIV positive or not. According to the CDC, it is estimated that one point two million individuals in the United States are living with diagnosed or undiagnosed HIV. HIV status awareness has risen by 86 to 87 percent between 2018 and 2022, but disparities remain because of stigma, false information, and unequal access to health services. These barriers will be discussed and illustrated during the seminar.

The presentation in the event will be by the inverted pyramid writing and presentation method- proceeding with the most vital information first- to reflect the way the health reporters structure their stories. The participants will know who is most impacted by the existing trends, the most efficient preventive measures to use, where local testing services can be found, when they should be tested, and why awareness is crucial to the young population.

A seminar session will be used to point out the social and economic injustices that contribute to the HIV disparities. The organizers will also underline the factors of fairness and inclusivity, whereby the prevention and testing programs will be extended to all student groups irrespective of background. The remaining sessions will be on how educational facilities will create confidential and stigma-free testing conditions that will facilitate the early detection and care.

Universal Intellectual Standards will be implemented by making residential seminar content straightforward, simple, and comprehensible, valid by using reliable CDC statistics, pertinent by focusing on juvenile health, and comprehensive by including the views of various communities.

The recommendations include the expansion of access to tests of confidentiality on campus, HIV education to be included in school health education, and peer-led prevention programs. These steps will contribute to the Healthy People 2030 objective of increasing the number of those who know their HIV status.

The aim of the seminar is not merely to pass information but also to create community interest. They will be provided with educational resources, advice on local prevention services, and actions to arrange school-based awareness programs. The event will try to substitute fear with empowerment by spreading accurate information and by encouraging open conversation and making HIV testing a normal aspect of health care.

"Expanding HIV testing, PrEP, and treatment have been effective, but progress must be faster to end new HIV infections. We must focus on addressing inequities and their drivers, including racism and social determinants of health. Whole person approaches to prevention, care, and treatment must reach all people equitably."

Robyn Neblett Fanfair, MD, MPH, CDC
