Pretesting your Brochure

(Used with the created brochure: "Caring for Your Mind in Later Life")

Target Audience:

The pretest involved five older adults (50-65 years old) who attended a community center in the local area. They were given a printed copy of the brochure and asked the following questions individually.

1. What is the first thing that caught your eye when you first saw this piece?

The participants reported that what drew their attention was the title Caring for Your Mind in Later Life and the smiling older adults in the picture first. They referenced the design as being relaxed, welcoming, and readable.

2. What do you think its main message is?

Every participant appropriately defined it by stating the primary message, that mental health in old age needs to be taken care of, and that it is as important as bodily health.

3. Does it raise your interest in the subject?

Yes. The participants indicated that the brochure increased their passion to know more about emotional well-being, in particular, how they could manage stress and loneliness over time.

4. Do you think the wording is appropriate for the target audience?

Yes. The language was said to be admirable, courteous, and clear. One participant was pleased that it was written in non-technical medical language.

5. What improvements would you make in its layout?

Participants suggested:

  • Adding a slightly larger font size for easier reading.
  • Including more spacing between bullet points.
  • Use softer background colors to reduce glare.

6. What could be added or removed to improve it?

Suggestions included:

  • Adding a short contact number for local mental-health support.
  • Including one positive quote or encouragement line on the back panel.
  • No information was found unnecessary; participants liked that it was "not too crowded."

7. Is the reading level compatible with that of the target population?

Yes. The level of reading was found to be easy and comprehensible to the older generation. Individuals reported that they did not need any assistance to read and comprehend it.

8. Is the format (channel) appropriate for the target audience?

Yes. It was considered that a printed tri-fold brochure was quite appropriate because most of the elderly adults consider reading at a slow pace and holding the piece that they are able to read.

9. How does the piece measure up to the Universal Intellectual Standards?

Participants noted the brochure was:

  • Clear: Simple wording and good flow.
  • Accurate: Information matched what doctors and TV programs say.
  • Relevant: Focused on senior experiences.
  • Fair: Positive and respectful toward older people.

Summary of Recommended Changes Based on Pretest

Participants recommended that the font size be enlarged and that the line spacing be made large to facilitate easier reading before final distribution. Their other suggestions were to have a pastel background, reduce glare, include a local mental-health contact number, and have an inspiring quote like It's never too late to take care of your mind. In general, the brochure was reported to be clear, appealing, and encouraging to the aged adults, who indicated that they would be happy distributing it to those within their age group..

Key Recommendations:

  1. Increase font size slightly for easier readability.
  2. Add more spacing between lines and bullet points for visual comfort.
  3. Use a light beige or pastel background instead of white to reduce glare.
  4. Include a local mental-health contact number or resource for quick help.
  5. Add an encouraging quote such as "It's never too late to care for your mind."

Overall, participants found the brochure clear, motivating, and visually appealing, and said they would be willing to share it with others in their age group.

Conclusion

The pretest findings indicated that the message, tone, and design used in the brochure are appropriate for older adults. Minor adjustments regarding visual and layout changes are required. The brochure is effective in fostering a mental well-being attitude in later life, which achieves the affective learning goal.
