Pretesting your Brochure

(Used with the created brochure: “Know Your HIV Status — Protect Your Health")

Target Audience:

In the pretest, five high school and college students (ages 16-21) from a local youth center were used. A copy of the brochure was printed, and each participant answered the following questions separately..

1. What is the first thing that caught your eye when you first saw this piece?

The participants were told that at first they paid attention to the name Know Your HIV Status and smiling students on campus. Their design was characterized as modern, clean, and easy to read, which is suitable for teens and younger adults.

2. What do you think its main message is?

Every participant accurately described that the brochure uses language to prompt the youth to be tested for HIV and appreciate why it is important to know the status of their health and safety. According to them, the message was direct and well articulated.

3. Does it raise your interest in the subject?

Yes. According to students, the brochure made them more interested in learning more about HIV, testing, and prevention. Others said that the brochure decreased confusion or fear regarding HIV testing and made the subject more comfortable.

4. Do you think the wording is appropriate for the target audience?

Yes. The participants said the language was easy to understand, not confrontational, and friendly. They were glad that the medical terminology was made simple and that the brochure was respectful of the experiences of students.

5. What improvements would you make in its layout?

Participants suggested:

  • Adding a slightly larger font size for easier reading.
  • Including more spacing between bullet points.
  • Use softer background colors to reduce glare.

6. What could be added or removed to improve it?

Suggestions included:

  • Inserting a QR code into a local HIV testing locator.
  • A list of temporary list of free testing sites for students.
  • Not even one of the participants believed that any information could be taken away; the brochure was described as being clear and not crowded.”

7. Is the reading level compatible with that of the target population?

Yes. According to students, the level of reading was suitable for both college and high school students. It was reported that everyone understood the brochure without any help.

8. Is the format (channel) appropriate for the target audience?

Yes. A tri-fold brochure in printed format was considered as a suitable mode since flyers, campus events, health office are the common channels by which the students get information about their health. They also commented that it is simple to carry and consult in the future.

9. How does the piece measure up to the Universal Intellectual Standards?

Participants noted the brochure was:

  • Clear: Simple wording and good flow.
  • Accurate: Information matched what doctors and TV programs say.
  • Relevant: Focused on senior experiences.
  • Fair: Positive and respectful toward older people.

Summary of Recommended Changes Based on Pretest

Participants recommended that the font size be enlarged and that the line spacing be made large to facilitate easier reading before final distribution. Their other suggestions were to have a pastel background, reduce glare, include a local mental-health contact number, and have an inspiring quote like It's never too late to take care of your mind. In general, the brochure was reported to be clear, appealing, and encouraging to the aged adults, who indicated that they would be happy distributing it to those within their age group..

Key Recommendations:

  1. Increase font size slightly for easier readability.
  2. Add more spacing between lines and bullet points for visual comfort.
  3. Use a light beige or pastel background instead of white to reduce glare.
  4. Include a local mental-health contact number or resource for quick help.
  5. Add an encouraging quote such as "Knowing your status gives you confidence and control"

On the whole, the participants were satisfied with the clarity, motivation, and appearance of the brochure, as well as declared their readiness to share it with their peers in their age group.

Conclusion

The pretest findings reveal that the message, tone, and design of the brochure are the right combination for the high school and college students. Minor visual and layout improvements are needed. The brochure effectively generates awareness in relation to HIV testing and supports the cognitive learning objective, which is influencing positive health decisions among the young people.
